Implementing Graphs

Written By Ved Panse Last Modified: 30 June 2021

In this section of the documentation, we will be looking at a very easy concept called graphs, and their use in writing efficient algorithmic programs.

What is a Graph?

The term "graph" used in this section is different from the graphs you might have learned in your school life as a part of Mathematics.

A more technical classification of it is done as an 'abstract data structure'. One can get confused between graphs and hash-tables quite easily.

Here's the difference:

Graphs Hash Tables
Graphs can establish a one-way or a two-way relationship. Hash-Tables establish only a one-way relationship.

That's it. That's the only difference that should set you.



One-Way relationship

Just like a hash-table, graphs can relate a key to a value. Thus, if you call out a key and print its output, you'll get the value associated with that key.

When a graph uses a one-way relationship, it is said to be an undirected graph.



Two-Way Relationship

Unlike a hash-table, the key-value pair in a graph can be used in any way. This means that you can input the key, get its associated value, and also the other way round. Such a graph is said to be a directed graph.



Two-Way Relationship General Example

Two-way relationships can be seen often in our daily life. For instance, when you shake hands with your friend, he shakes hands with you back. Let's take your friend's name to be Eric. When a third-person asks you who you shook hands with, your answer would be "Eric." Similarly, if that same third-person asks Eric the same question he asked you, he would answer taking your name.

Basically, both of you would be conveying the same message: "You shook hands with Eric" and "Eric shook hands with you."

This is the perfect example of a two-way relationship.



The Technical Side of Two-Way Relationships

In the real-time example stated above, the third person asked both you and Eric the same question. Thus, we can conclude that at least O(number of people involved) functions are required.

Above all, you'll also have to get rid of redundant code, which happens to be asking the same question to Eric and you both.



Worst Case Scenario

Let's say the third person gets redirected to the person with who one handshakes. In this case, he will ask Eric the question, then you. Then, he will get redirected back to Eric and this will lead to an infinite loop. This happens to be the worst-case scenario.

Thus, a proposable solution pseudocode is:- 




asked_list = ["Eric", "Sam", "Tom"]

def ask_question(name):
    if name in asked_list:
        print("Asked already.")
    else:
        ask() # Asks question
        asked_list.append(name)
