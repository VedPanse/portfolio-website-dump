Introduction to Algorithms

Written By Ved Panse Last Modified: 30 June 2021

In this section of the documentation, we will be looking at what an algorithm really is. Also, we will be looking at some of the misconception that attribute algorithms to be only a 'computer-related subject'.

You can do this!

What is an algorithm?

An algorithm is a set of instructions followed to accomplish a task. For instance, take a trip from your house to your school. It involves an algorithm of directions. You follow a particular code (set) of instructions to reach your destination. In this example, it is simply the directions (left, right, etc.). So, unknowingly, we all use algorithms in day-to-day life without even realizing it.

The brain is indeed a mystery. In the following lessons, I will be focussing on algorithms used to accomplish a task that you provide to your computer.

How are items stored in a computer?

Imagine a computer to be a bookshelf that is already filled with some books, but is not full. The books that already occupy the bookshelf are kept in a random order and not next to each other. Consider the items that you want to be stored in the bookshelf to be books. Further explanation has been split into two datatypes: arrays and lists to understand two different cases of memory storage.



Arrays Lists
  • In arrays, their elements are stored one after the other on the shelf, just like books, in the order specified.
  • Take, for example, that there is a shelf and you have 10 books to be stored. But, the maximum capacity for accommodating it in the bookshelf one after the other is seven (and the eighth position is occupied by something else).
  • The books cannot be stored in an array, thus leading to problems dealing with memory. The elements cannot be spread over the place, like in the case of lists.
  • In lists, their elements are stored in order, but not necessarily one after the other. The elements are spread over to occupy any given space.
  • Take for example that there are 10 books to be arranged. But, the maximum capacity for accommodating it in the bookshelf one after the other is seven (and the eighth position is occupied by something else).
  • In lists, the first seven elements get stored in the order provided, while the other elements occur after the seventh position. One element stores the link of the next one.
