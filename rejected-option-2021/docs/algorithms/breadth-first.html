Breadth First Search

Written By Ved Panse Last Modified: 30 June 2021

In this section of the documentation, we will be looking at breadth-first search: one of the most used algorithms in the world.

What is Breadth-First Search?

Breadth-first search can be described as one of those "Find the shortest distance to a restaurant" algorithms. You might have used one in Google Maps.



General Applications of Breadth-First Search

  1. Shortest moves to victory in Chess
  2. Restaurant within the closest proximity
  3. Spelling filter



General Example

Suppose you are in a cafe in New York, sipping coffee and planning your itinerary to The Statue of Liberty. Having an exhausting day, you want to shorten your itinerary to the minimum. Thus, you target the shortest walking distance to your destination. This is an application of the shortest path problem, that falls under Breadth-First search.



How would you solve the above problem?

Well, as a tourist to New York, it is most likely that you will be there for the first time. Thus, you will use your map to calculate your route to The Statue of Liberty.

  1. Open a map (graph).
  2. Calculate the shortest route using breadth-first search.



Graphs

The graphs used in this section of the documentation aren't those with the x and y axes, which you see in Mathematics.

They are made up of nodes and edges. A node can be directly or indirectly linked to multiple other nodes. The nodes that are directly connected to the selected node are called neighbor nodes.
Note: There can be only one selected node at a time.

Thus, the graphs used in this section are just a representation of how places or things are connected to one another.



Back to Breadth-First Search

We can say that breadth-first searches are examples of binary search. The only difference is that breadth-first search algorithms use graphs to find items.



Breadth-First Search: How it thinks

It systematically maps the following steps:-

  1. Is there a way to achieve Node B, if the selected node is Node A?
  2. If yes, what is the shortest distance between them?
  3. If no, then perform recursive breadth-first searches (described below)



Recursive Breadth-first searches

Suppose you work in a vacuum cleaner-producing company, but you have no idea where to start getting feedback and suggestions. Thus, you ask your friends for feedback on their vacuum cleaners.

On satisfying their suggestions, you also ask their friends for feedback and suggestions. Thus, by performing the above tasks recursively, you will achieve your goal. This is how breadth-first searches can be used recursively.



Shortest Paths

Let's call directly connected nodes 1st-degree connections and indirectly connected nodes as 2nd-degree connections.

In the above example, your friends and you are connected with 1st-degree connections, while your friends' friends and you are connected with 2nd-degree connections.

Note: 2nd-degree connections are not possible without 1st-degree connections.



Queue

Queues are data structures that primarily function based on the order of the elements in them.

Suppose you are before your friend in a queue in a shopping mall for checking out. First, you get to check out, and only after you, your friend can check out.

Queues function on the same analogy. In a queue, you cannot pick any element. You have to follow the order to find your element. Thus, only two operations are possible when working with queuing:



Enqueue Dequeue
Adding elements to a queue List/Array provided through callback function.


Considering the example of a queue in which your friend and you are standing, you will be dequeued and searched for before your friend.

Queues are commonly attributed to the abbreviations: FIFO and LIFO.



FIFO LIFO
First In First Out Last In First Out.
